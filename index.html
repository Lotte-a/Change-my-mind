Change My mind

See the difference you can make as a developer by developing accessible websites.

Hi, I am Jake.
I am one of the 300.000 people living in the Netherlands that have a visual impairment.

I can only see 25%. As a partially sighted person, I encounter a lot of problems when using various websites every day.

Problems (HTML)

1. Not completely accessible by keyboard.

Tabbable elements go through their default source order. This can cause major confusion. Use this only if it is really needed, for example, if the layout shows things in a very different visual order to the source code.

2. Bad content structure.

Headings, paragraphs etc. are written with presentational HTML and line breaks. The screenreader will not have antything to use as signpost, and reads the text out in one go. 

<font size="7">My heading</font> <br><br>This is the first section of my document.<br><br>

3. Vague labels of buttons and links.

The labels of buttons and links are vague. The screenreader will give confusing readouts. 

Fill in your name: <input type="text" id="name" name="name">

4. Page layouts created with HTML tables.

Page layouts created with HTML tabels. A screenreader will give out focusing readouts because of this. Especially when the layout is complex and has many nested tables. 

<table width="1200"><!-- main heading row --><tr id="heading"> <td colspan="6"> <h1 align="center">Header</h1> </td> </tr> </table>

5. No contrast option or contrast ratio of 3:1.

The text and background do not contain a minimum contrast ratio of 3:1. People with a visual-impairment will not be aible to see the difference between them because of this.

6. Missing or vague alt attributes at images.

The alt text is is vague or not provided in alt attributes wherever possible. Visually-impaired people do not know what is on the image/video content because of this. 

<img src="dinosaur.png">

I can only see 25%. As a partially sighted person, I encounter a lot of problems when using various websites every day.

Problems solved (HTML)

1. Completely accessible by keyboard.

The tabindex attribute is primarily intended to allow tabbable elements to have custom tab order (specified in positive numercial order). 

<div data-message="This is from the first button" tabindex="0">Click me!</div>

2. Clean content structure.

The content structure is clean with headings, paragraphs, lists etc. The screenreader reads out clear information and you can jump to the next/previous heading, to go through it faster. 

<h1>My heading</h1> <p>This is the first section of my document.</p> <ol> <li>Here is</li> <li>a list for</li> <li>you to read</li> </ol>

3. Clear labels of buttons and links.

The labels of buttons and links are clear. The screenreader will give clear readouts. Good code example: 

<div><label for="name">Fill in your name:</label><input type="text" id="name" name="name"></div>

4. Page layouts created without HTML tables.

The website contains a modern structure. The screnreader readouts clear information. 

<header> <h1>Header</h1> </header> <nav><!-- main navigation in here --></nav> <!-- Here is our page's main content --> <main> <!-- It contains an article --> <article> </article>

5. Contrast option or contrast ratio of 3:1.

The background and the text do contain the minimum contrast ratio of 3:1.

6. Clear alt attributes at images.

Alt text is clear and provided in alt attributes where possible.This way people with a visual-impairment can also understand what the image/video content contains. 

<img src="dinosaur.png" alt="A red Tyrannosaurus Rex: A two legged dinosaur standing upright like a human, with small arms, and a large head with lots of sharp teeth.">

Due those problems, I am unable to independently use a website.This gives me the feeling that I am not a part of the society.

You as a developer can solve this problem!

By correctly implementing the WCAG guidelines in the development of a website, you can make websites accessible for people with the same disabilities as me and for all people with other disabilities in the world!
